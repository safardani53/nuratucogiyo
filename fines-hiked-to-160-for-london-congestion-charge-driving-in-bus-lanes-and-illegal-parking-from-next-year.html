Fines hiked to 160 for London Congestion Charge, driving in bus lanes and illegal parking from nex

January 2024 · 3 minute read

FINES for driving in bus lanes and failing to pay the Congestion Charge will rocket to £160 from next year.

Transport for London (TfL) revealed penalties will soar from January 2 2018 for bus lane offences and late congestion charge payments.

Fines for illegal parking and turns plus blocking red routes are likely to be hiked later that month, too, pending government sign off.

Other councils are expected to follow suit later in the year with TfL raking in an extra £20m a year from the changes.

Fines rise to £160 from the existing £130 while early payment discount rates hike from £65 to £80.

The level of fine compares with £60 for an eight-hour day on the current National Living Wage while pensioners face losing two-thirds of their state pension if they break the rules.

And it's more than first-time shoplifters (up to £100 in value) and those who give a false alarm to the emergency services (£90). While vandals are only fined up to £300.

Edmund King, AA president, said: "Congestion charge and moving traffic fines need to be a proportionate deterrent. Many people will consider that £80, let alone £160, is not.

"Our main concern is the lack of discretion. There will still be drivers who may stray into the congestion zone or a bus lane and come straight out.

"Would a short grace period or warning letter for the first time not be a fairer response?

"Knowing how mercilessly fines are pursued, the AA would argue that a driver who has to go through a lengthy appeal and wins should be compensated the full £160 PCN amount for the hassle and false accusation.

"The aspect that worries us most is other councils seeing the riches of PCNs at this level and wanting the same."

TfL said the change was needed after Penalty Charge Notices soared by 12 per cent in the last five years to 1.5million.

The fine increase comes months after TfL increased the daily C-Charge to £11.50 - while in October it introduced a new £10 a day diesel tax dubbed the T-Charge.

TfL said motorists blocking red routes and stopping cars turning were increasing congestion and pollution in the capital.

Paul Cowperthwaite, TfL's general manager for road user charging, said: "We want to make London's streets safer and healthier places that are less dominated by the car.

"Although the Congestion Charge has been effective in reducing the number of cars entering central London, we've seen a 12 per cent increase in the number of motorists being issued with PCNs in the last five years.

"This shows that the deterrent factor of the existing PCN has reduced over time.

"The new PCN level will help improve compliance and also encourage people to consider cheaper and more active alternative forms of travel."
